Where ordinary workflows become the dominant security risk.

A cybersecurity and privacy risk assessment of a cloud-based healthcare analytics environment spanning SurveyToGo, SNAP 11, Azure SQL, GetThematic, Power BI, and a governed reporting portal. The dominant residual risks in this environment are not dramatic external attacks — they are ordinary workflow failures at trust boundaries between systems.

Key findings

i
The dominant threat is workflow failure, not platform compromise. Stale access after role change, row-level-security errors at release, stale PDF recipient lists, wrong-identifiability uploads to GetThematic, and over-scoped integration identities produce more plausible harm than external attack on Azure or Microsoft cloud controls.
ii
Two trust boundaries concentrate the residual exposure. Azure SQL → GetThematic (identifiability decisions, manual transfer methods) and the reporting/export surface (where governance weakens after data leaves the portal) account for the majority of High and Extreme ratings.
iii
Free text is a structurally distinct risk class. Contextual identifiers persist in qualitative responses even where structured fields are pseudonymised, which is why R7 engages the NDB scheme independent of any structured-data failure.
iv
The future-state design is deliberately targeted, not comprehensive. Three more ambitious directions — architectural decomposition of the integration path, programmatic redaction of free text, and deeper warehouse tenant segregation — were considered and explicitly set aside as belonging to a separate architectural conversation.
§ 02

Current-state architecture

4 min

An Australia-hosted SaaS and Azure ecosystem that collects, stores, transforms, analyses, and reports healthcare survey, audit, and qualitative feedback data. Boundaries 3 and 5 are the most sensitive transition points and account for the majority of High and Extreme residual ratings.

Figure 1 · System flow and trust boundaries Hover a boundary to reveal linked risks

Six named components sit within the assessment scope. SurveyToGo and SNAP 11 are upstream SaaS source systems, feeding Azure SQL through API. Client-level databases are maintained before consolidation into the reporting warehouse. Qualitative text flows from Azure SQL into GetThematic via API, export, or manual upload depending on client configuration — a single boundary with three different strength levels. Power BI connects directly to Azure SQL and, for some clients, to GetThematic. End users consume reporting through a governed password-protected portal with row-level security.

A seventh cross-cutting plane — identity, vendor support, and privileged administration — spans all components and is treated as Boundary 6 in the threat model.

§ 03

Threat model

3 min

The threat picture is shaped less by direct compromise of a cloud platform and more by the interaction between privileged access, exportable reporting outputs, client-managed recipient lifecycle, free-text sensitivity, integration identities, and inconsistent control strength across multiple transfer paths. Five threat classes organise the register.

TC1
Authorised-user overreach

Legitimate access becomes harmful when permissions remain broader than operational need after role or project change. The system works as built; the permission model has drifted since last review.

TC2
Configuration failure

Normal release or modelling activity introduces disclosure through incorrect row-level security, audience mapping, or segregation logic. No malicious action required — routine development without regression testing.

TC3
Integration identity misuse

An API key, service account, or machine credential is compromised or overused. Blast radius is high because one identity can traverse Azure SQL, GetThematic, and Power BI in a single call chain.

TC4
Qualitative privacy leakage

Free text or metadata re-identifies people through contextual detail. Free-text responses contain role names, locations, and incident descriptions that persist even where structured fields are pseudonymised.

TC5
Workflow escape

Governed data leaves the governed environment through export, forwarding, copying, or stale automated distribution. The initial event can be alerted, but downstream handling is outside direct control.

Amplifier logic
Two register entries — R12 (informal incident ownership) and R13 (inconsistent log-review evidence) — are treated as amplifiers rather than primary disclosure pathways. Their effect is on containment speed and detectability across every other threat class during a live incident.

§ 06

ISO 27001 / ISMS gap assessment

4 min

ISO 27001:2022 Annex A provides the control vocabulary; the remaining gaps are concentrated at operational transition points rather than within any single platform. Click a domain to expand the current position and linked risks.

A.5.15 – A.5.18 · A.8.2 – A.8.3 Access control & dual-surface governance Open +

Baseline controls are strong — SSO, MFA, governed portal access, RLS, centrally managed provisioning, formal JML processes. The weakness is not absence of control but privilege drift and configuration dependence across the separate Power BI and GetThematic role models.

A.5.18 compliance depends on cross-platform reconciliation that is not yet formalised for mover events. A mover ticket correctly processed in one platform can leave residual access in the other.

Linked risks R1 R9
A.5.12 – A.5.14 · A.8.12 Information classification & transfer Open +

The four-tier classification model, project-specific identifiability rules, siloed re-identification keys, restricted raw free-text access, and client-specific minimisation rules are strong governance features. The main weakness is that classification cannot be treated as static — it must be re-applied at every transfer and output point.

A.8.12 data-leakage controls are partly implemented but not yet consistently evidenced at every export path, and exports are often classified differently from source data.

Linked risks R3 R5 R7
A.5.19 – A.5.23 Supplier & third-party governance Closed · monitor +

Supplier assurance is comparatively mature through executed contracts, DPAs, privacy clauses, security schedules, and formal vendor-access approval processes. GetThematic remains a sensitive supplier boundary because qualitative text and metadata cross into third-party SaaS.

The specific A.5.23 evidence gap has now been closed — the current independent assurance artefact has been formally reviewed by the CTO and recorded in the supplier review pack. Ongoing monitoring under A.5.22 remains necessary.

Linked risks R11
A.8.15 · A.8.16 Logging, monitoring & event review Partly implemented +

Daily review, alerting, central handling of noteworthy events, and a defined retention schedule give this domain a strong baseline. The remaining gap is no longer the absence of rules but the need to apply them consistently across all platforms and retain evidence that log review, alert handling, and exception review have actually occurred — the heart of A.8.16 assurance.

Linked risks R13 R3 R6 R7
A.5.24 – A.5.28 Incident management Open +

Runbooks for revoked access, wrong-file uploads, export leaks, and credential compromise support incident readiness. The remaining weakness is that control ownership is understood informally and can shift with business structure; in a time-critical incident, that flexibility can blur who has final authority over Power BI exposure, GetThematic disclosure, or wrong-recipient events.

Linked risks R12
A.5.34 · A.8.12 Healthcare-data & privacy handling Open +

Healthcare-data risk remains concentrated in free-text disclosure, export beyond the governed environment, and inconsistent control strength where transfers become manual or client-dependent. Compliance depends on minimisation, approval checks, restrictions around raw text, and client-governed retention exceptions being applied consistently across the Azure SQL → GetThematic → Power BI pathway.

Australian regulatory overlay: Privacy Act 1988 (APP 11, APP 6) and the Notifiable Data Breaches scheme operate alongside Annex A and impose independent obligations. R3, R4, and R7 engage the NDB serious-harm threshold under s 26WK.

Linked risks R3 R4 R5 R7
A.5.17 · A.8.24 · A.8.32 Change management & secrets Open +

Privileged change controls are mature through peer review, change approval, admin logging, and an emergency-change register. Secrets management shows a defined register, rotation process, and CTO oversight. The residual gap is that a small number of privileged roles still hold high-consequence power across multiple systems, and permission regression testing is not yet consistently required as a first-class release control after every material reporting change.

§ 07

Future-state design

3 min

Deliberately targeted rather than comprehensive. Three waves, sequenced so detection and ownership controls are strengthened against an already-tightened control baseline rather than in parallel.

Wave 1 · Top priority 26 Apr 2026

Outward-facing disclosure pathways

The most direct exposure routes to unintended recipients and the clearest engagement with NDB obligations. High value, low structural change, and they unblock assurance evidence for the amplifier controls downstream.

  • 1aExport governance — weekly alert review, justified high-sensitivity exports, tightened role matrixR3 · R13
  • 1bQuarterly recipient recertification and distribution-list hygieneR4
  • 1cCross-platform access reconciliation and mover control (Power BI ↔ GetThematic)R1 · R9
  • 1dPermission regression testing as a formal release gate for audience-affecting changesR2 · R10
Wave 2 · Structural 12 May 2026

Integration identity & qualitative-data handling

Higher coordination cost but attacks the root of the most consequential misuse pathways. Scope reduction of machine identities, identifiability sign-off on qualitative transfers, and raw-text access narrowing.

  • 2aUpload validation checklist with identifiability sign-off before every qualitative transferR5
  • 2bRaw-text access reduction and output-masking decision recordR7
  • 2cAPI / service identity scope review plus anomaly thresholdsR6
Wave 3 · Amplifier controls 11 Jun 2026

Detection, ownership & supplier review

Amplifier controls deliver most value once primary disclosure paths are tightened. Strengthens containment speed, detectability, and evidentiary quality of incident response.

  • 3aDocumented incident ownership matrix with tabletop exercise evidenceR12
  • 3bPlatform-wide log-review evidence and recall testingR13
  • 3cSupplier assurance renewal cadence and review retentionR11
Explicitly excluded
Architectural decomposition of the integration path (would reduce R6 blast radius below the High band — requires technical discovery beyond the evidence base). Programmatic redaction of free text (lossy for qualitative analysis — belongs upstream of the treatment plan). Deeper warehouse tenant segregation (would strengthen R8 — sits outside the risk-treatment horizon). Each is legitimate; each belongs to a separate architectural conversation.

Interpretation

What this model is not
The simulator takes each treatment's target residual from the register at face value and assumes independent effect on its mapped risks. It does not model partial implementation, control-quality variance, or second-order interactions. Its purpose is to surface the shape of the treatment plan — what it addresses, what it leaves untouched, and where the floors are — not to produce a point estimate of residual risk. R8 (cross-client visibility after warehouse merging) receives no treatment in this plan and remains fixed at its current residual.
§ 09

Methodological critique

2 min

ISO 27001 / Annex A is valuable as a control vocabulary but obscures engineering depth when compliance citation is treated as technical sufficiency. The stronger assessment uses Annex A as organising structure while testing each clause against the architecture's actual failure modes.

Worked example — A.5.18 (access rights)

The same three-layer logic — citation · technical test · evidence — can be applied to every Annex A domain in this report. It is what distinguishes a compliance-mapped assessment from an engineering-grounded one.

01 · Citation

A.5.18 requires that access rights be provisioned, reviewed, modified, and removed per policy. The environment has a JML process, governed approvals, and periodic access reviews. The clause can be cited with confidence.

02 · Technical test

The clause does not speak to the specific failure mode here: Power BI and GetThematic maintain separate role models. A mover event correctly processed in one platform can leave residual access in the other. The engineering question is whether the JML workflow requires a dual-platform check.

03 · Evidence

Defensibility rests on artefacts the clause does not specify: a JML ticket showing both platforms were updated, a cross-platform reconciliation record, and a sign-off confirming the dual check was performed. R1 and R9 remain open because the gap is beneath the clause, not within it.
